How to spot your eye shape — and which lash actually fits.

Lash fit is the difference between a band that sits flush against your lash line and one perched halfway up your lid like a tiny ladder. Three eye shapes, ten lashes, no guesswork.

Why eye shape matters more than face shape

Beauty media tends to obsess over face shape — heart, oval, square, the rest. That's useful for haircuts. It's almost useless for lashes. A magnetic lash band only touches one thing on your face: the line where your top lashes meet your lid. Whether the band sits flush or floats off depends entirely on the geometry of that one line.

For magnetic lashes, three eye-shape categories cover roughly 90% of the Malaysian population. They're not exotic — they're just rarely explained in the brands designed in LA or Seoul.

Find yours in 30 seconds

Stand in front of a mirror in good light. Look straight ahead, both eyes open, relaxed. Don't try to widen them — just normal.

Now look at your upper lid crease: the fold that appears when your eye opens.

1. Monolid

You don't see a crease at all. The lid skin runs in a smooth curve from your lash line up to your brow bone. The lash line itself is often partly tucked under the lid skin, and lashes tend to point downward or straight out rather than up.

What works: bands with a high-lift curl (70° rather than 50°), shorter base length, and a thinner band profile so it doesn't pile up against the lid skin.

Try: Pitch (the 70° L arc was literally designed for this), Minutes (lightest weight, true-to-lash), Nightshift (fox-eye upswept, great for evening).

2. Double lid

You see a defined crease that runs roughly parallel to your lash line, with a visible strip of lid skin between the lash line and the crease. Your lashes naturally curl upward. This is the eye shape most Western brands assume everyone has.

What works: almost everything — but you can wear the bolder, longer styles without them touching the crease. The visible lid space means longer lashes look proportional.

Try: Afterhours (sunflower triangle cluster, peak drama), Memo (7-cluster fairy burst for a wider-eyed look), VIP Access (statement length).

3. Inner double lid

You have a crease, but it's partial — visible only at the outer half of your lid, and the inner corner is closer to monolid. Your lash line is partly visible in the middle and outer third, hidden at the inner corner.

This is the most common eye shape across Southeast Asia, and the one Western lash designs handle worst — they tend to be cut for a fully-visible lid and the band sits too tall at the inner corner.

What works: bands with a tapered inner end (thinner band at the inner corner, full at the outer), shorter inner-corner fibres, medium-lift curl.

Try: Minutes (true-to-lash simulation that respects the partial lid), Pitch (Monolid / Inner-double-lid favourite), Kickoff (Korean strand-by-strand designed for partial-lid geometry).

If you don't fit cleanly into one

Plenty of people have hybrid eyes — monolid on one side and a partial inner double on the other, or eyes that change with age (we all eventually get a touch more hooded). For these, two approaches work:

  1. Pick by the lower-energy side. If one eye is more monolid, pick the lash designed for that. The double-lid eye will still wear it well; the reverse isn't usually true.
  2. Try the universal styles. Some bands are explicitly designed to work across all three shapes — Inbox, Kickoff, Boardroom, Twilight, and VIP Access all fall into that category.

One more thing — band trimming

If your eye is on the smaller side, even the right lash style might be a fraction too long. All Santai bands are designed to be trimmed at the outer corner with regular nail scissors — measure against your natural lash line, mark gently with the tip of an applicator, and clip 1-2 lashes off the outer end. That fixes 90% of fit issues for shorter eye lengths.
